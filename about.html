About · Why it exists · 25 April 2026

A solo project, shipped in a fortnight, on a stack that shouldn't have been possible.

How {things} Work is a one-person project. I'm Colin Burns, a software engineer based in Melbourne. I built this app over two weekends — about twenty hours of focused work — and the result is what you'd normally expect from a small team running for a quarter. An iOS app, two backend services, a content pipeline, self-hosted infrastructure, full CI/CD. Twelve months ago this would have been an ambitious year-long side project. Six months ago, a tough quarter. Today: two weekends.

The honest motivation

There used to be a moat around the industry. Building anything substantial — a real application, with a backend, a database, a deployment pipeline, a mobile app, a content system — was hard, and the difficulty was the reason the work was paid the way it was. Years of experience went into knowing which patterns to reach for, which mistakes to avoid, and how all the pieces fit together.

That moat is shrinking, and that turns out to be the most exciting thing to happen to software in my career. The constraints I grew up with — the tedious month of boilerplate before you got to the interesting work, the sheer typing tax of building something end-to-end — those are dissolving. What's left is the part I always liked: deciding what to build, deciding how it should be put together, deciding where the failure modes hide.

I won't pretend it's not also unsettling. Every software engineer I know is feeling some version of the same conflict: I can ship things I never could before, sitting awkwardly next to and what does that mean for the way I make a living? Both are true. Both are worth taking seriously. This project was, partly, my way of looking at the conflict directly instead of around it.

Why a "how things work" app

The premise comes from a science-fiction trope I've always loved. Someone travels back in time and finds they can name the things from the future — microwave, satellite, insulin — but can't actually explain any of them. The mechanics are gone the moment they leave their century.

I wanted a tool for that. A pocket reference for the everyday machinery of modern life, written plainly, short enough to read in a queue, working offline. Not a survival manual, not prepper content — I wanted curiosity, well-served, not a doomsday framing. And honestly, I also wanted an excuse to learn iOS development properly. New platform, real project, no tutorials.

Twelve months ago this would have been a year-long side project. Today: two weekends.

How it actually got built

The structure of the work was driven by a product requirements document. I didn't type the PRD any more than I typed the code — I coordinated its development through conversation with the AI, the same way I coordinated the development of everything else. Treating the document as "real work I did" while treating the code as "the AI's work" would have been a strange line to draw.

From the PRD, Taskmaster broke the work down into discrete, ordered tasks with dependencies. Claude Code then picked up tasks one at a time, wrote the implementations, and handed each piece back for review. My job was architecture, judgement calls, security review, and saying "no, that's not the trade-off I want."

The design followed the same pattern. I'm not a designer. I think I can tell good design from bad, but I can't produce good design from a blank page — there's a real difference between recognising taste and having the hands to express it. Halfway through the project, Claude Design was released, and that gave me the missing third of the team.

The app's visual system started as a brief I worked out in a Claude conversation: an "academic atelier" direction, warm and editorial, deliberately rejecting both sterile EdTech and gamified cartoonishness. I iterated on screens with Claude Design until the look felt right. For this marketing site, I asked it for eight variations against the app's visual language; this is the one I picked. A few of the others looked more aggressively editorial — newspaper-like, dense — but they came with hallucinated copy I'd have had to audit and rewrite end-to-end. This one wore its constraints more gently.

The stack

Mobile

iOS app (React Native)

Built with Expo and React Native, offline-first via SQLite. Targeting iOS for now; Android falls out cheaply when I'm ready. The first mobile app I've shipped.

Backend

API + content pipeline

Two services: a Fastify API serving the app, and a content pipeline that drafts, edits, and ships explainers using LLMs with a retrieval layer.

Infrastructure

Self-hosted, end-to-end

Terraform-provisioned droplet, self-hosted Postgres and Redis, DO Spaces with a CDN for images. Tailscale for everything administrative.

Delivery

Custom CI/CD

A self-hosted GitHub Actions runner on a NAS at home, pushing images to a private container registry. Portainer manages deploys to the droplet via an Edge Agent.

What this actually felt like

The honest verdict: extraordinary, with footnotes. The AI did not replace the engineering — architecture, storage strategy, security model, deployment topology, the dozens of little judgement calls that decide whether a system holds up under load — those were decisions a human still has to make, and the AI was wrong often enough that you notice. What it replaced, almost completely, was the typing. The line-by-line grind. Afternoons that used to disappear into boilerplate now compress into review sessions.

The moat hasn't disappeared. It's moved. Scott Werner has a wonderful piece called Warranty Void If Regenerated that imagines what software work looks like a few years into this transition: a country mechanic, working out of a corrugated steel shop, diagnosing the gap between what farmers specified and what their generated tools actually do. The skill that pays the bills isn't typing code. It's reading specs against reality, choreographing how generated tools interact, and translating the kind of knowledge that lives in someone's hands into language a machine can act on. It's a piece of fiction, but it doesn't feel far off.

That's roughly what building this app felt like, in miniature. The leverage is real. The work just looks different now.

What's next

I'm planning to open-source the repository once I've finished a more targeted security review. The interesting artefact isn't the app itself — it's the shape of the project: what a solo developer can architect and ship right now, with the right tools and a clear plan. If even one person reads this and thinks I could build the thing I've been putting off, the page has earned its keep.
