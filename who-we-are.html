About · Who we are

A small team writing plainly about complicated things.

How {things} Work is a reader-supported field guide to the machinery of modern life. We make short, fact-checked explainers about the everyday objects, systems, and ideas that quietly run the world — and we try very hard not to be boring about it.

Why we made this

Most of us live surrounded by technology we don't really understand. The microwave on the counter, the satellite that just routed our morning meeting, the medication in the cabinet, the engine outside the airplane window. All of them are knowable. They were designed by people, and people can explain them.

The catch is that most explanations are either too shallow to be useful or written in a register that assumes you already speak the dialect. We wanted a guide that sits between those — short, honest, occasionally illustrated, and willing to name the place where the simple version stops being true.

So we made one. Roughly four hundred topics, organised across seven categories, each one edited until it can be read in under three minutes by someone with no background in the subject. It is offline, ad-free, and free forever. We are funded by a small recurring donation programme and a few institutional supporters who do not get a say in what we cover.

How we write

01

Plain English first.

If a sentence requires a glossary, we rewrite the sentence. Specialist vocabulary is fine — but only after we've earned it.

02

Show where it breaks.

Every analogy stretches thin somewhere. We point out the spot, in the same piece, before someone has to correct us.

03

Source, then publish.

Everything is anchored to peer-reviewed work, primary documents, or named experts. The source list is on the page, not buried.

04

Corrections in public.

If we get something wrong, we fix it and log the change with a date stamp at the bottom of the topic. Readers' names credited.

Funding & independence

We do not run advertising and we do not sell reader data. Operating costs are covered by: a recurring donation programme (about 4,200 monthly supporters as of this writing); a handful of grants from non-partisan science-communication foundations; and modest revenue from print editions of selected categories.

No funder has editorial input. The full list of supporters above £2,000 per year is on the contact page, alongside our annual financial summary.

If you'd like to write for us, suggest a topic, or flag an error, the contact page has the right address for each.
